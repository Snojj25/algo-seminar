%% Beats: (1) IVM-for-cyclic-joins framing, (2) state of the art (triangles tight,
%% 4-cliques tight, 4-cycles long believed O(m^{2/3})), (3) Assadi-Shah's

Counting subgraphs in a fully dynamic graph is used in databases
for incremental view maintenance (IVM) of cyclic joins. The
worst-case update time is settled for two of the three patterns on
at most four vertices: triangles at $O(\sqrt{m})$, $4$-cliques at
$O(m)$. For $4$-cycles the previous upper bound was
$O(m^{2/3})$~\cite{HHH22}; Assadi and
Shah~\cite{AssadiShah25} recently improved this to
$O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon})$ for a constant $\varepsilon > 0$ using
fast matrix multiplication (FMM), a tool previously thought
inapplicable to dynamic IVM. This essay re-presents their algorithm
through the IVM-for-cyclic-joins lens and contributes (i) a
closed-form algebraic certificate that the underlying constraint
system is feasible at the conjectural $\omega = 2$ with explicit
rational parameters, complementing the numerical verification
of~\cite{AssadiShah25}; and (ii) an empirical study contrasting
the warm-up algorithm of~\cite[\S3]{AssadiShah25} (with NumPy GEMM
substituting for FMM) against a layer-aware Simple-Wedge baseline,
which locates the input regime --- bilateral hubs on both sides of
the cyclic ordering --- where the class-routing machinery actually
pays off.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Counting occurrences of a small pattern graph (triangles, $4$-cycles,
$4$-cliques) inside a larger host graph is one of the oldest and
most broadly applicable problems in algorithmic graph theory. The
same primitive underpins clustering and community-detection
heuristics in social-network analysis~\cite{SMS20}, motif-discovery
pipelines in computational biology~\cite{MSOI02}, and
recurring-subexpression evaluation in database query
processing~\cite{RPS21}.

A further motivation, central to this paper, comes from database
theory: \emph{incremental view maintenance} (IVM) for \emph{cyclic
joins}. A cyclic join is a query $R_1 \bowtie R_2 \bowtie \cdots
\bowtie R_k$ over $k$ binary relations whose attribute schemas form
a cycle, with each relation sharing one attribute with its predecessor
and one with its successor, and the last relation closing back to
the first. Such queries are a classical hard case for join
evaluation~\cite{NRR14,NPRR18}. The
four-way instance considered throughout this paper is
\[
  J \;=\; A(L_1,L_2) \bowtie B(L_2,L_3) \bowtie C(L_3,L_4) \bowtie D(L_4,L_1),
\]
which has a clean graph-theoretic reading: each binary relation is
a bipartite edge set between two attribute domains, and $|J|$
equals the number of $4$-cycles in the resulting $4$-layered graph
(Section~\ref{sec:reduction:joins}). IVM updates a query's answer
incrementally after each tuple change; maintaining $|J|$ under
updates is therefore equivalent to dynamic $4$-cycle counting
(Section~\ref{sec:reduction}).

In each of these settings the host graph evolves under edge
insertions and deletions, and recomputing the count from scratch
is infeasible at scale. This motivates the \emph{fully dynamic}
model of subgraph counting, in which the exact count must be
reported after every update; we measure cost by the
\emph{worst-case update time}, the maximum work on any single
update, which is strictly stronger than amortised time.

The complexity of dynamic subgraph counting on small patterns is
well understood for two of the three patterns on at most four
vertices, summarised in Table~\ref{tab:ladder}. Triangles have
worst-case update time $\Theta(\sqrt{m})$~\cite{KNN20,HKNS15}
(the lower bound is conditional on the OMv conjecture);
$4$-cliques have complexity $\Theta(m)$ for combinatorial
algorithms under the $4$-clique
conjecture~\cite{HHH22}. The $4$-cycle lies between these two
extremes: Hanauer, Henzinger, and Hua~\cite{HHH22} obtained
$O(m^{2/3})$ by generalising the triangle bound's
wedge-maintenance idea, with the only matching lower bound the
$\Omega(m^{1/2-\gamma})$ inherited from triangles.

Assadi and Shah~\cite{AssadiShah25} prove that the $O(m^{2/3})$
upper bound is not tight. They give an algorithm with worst-case
update time $O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon})$ for a constant $\varepsilon >
0$ depending on the matrix-multiplication exponent $\omega$, the
smallest constant for which two $n \times n$ matrices can be
multiplied in $O(n^\omega)$ time. The current best is
$\omega < 2.371339$~\cite{ADW25}, yielding $\varepsilon \approx
0.0098$; under the conjectural optimum $\omega = 2$ the bound
improves to $\varepsilon = 1/24$. The result is conceptually surprising: fast
matrix multiplication (FMM) was previously believed incompatible
with dynamic IVM (Section~\ref{sec:related:fmm-belief}), and the
Online Matrix-Vector multiplication (OMv) lower bound
of~\cite{HKNS15} rules out only combinatorial improvements, leaving
room for an algebraic speedup~\cite[\S1]{AssadiShah25}.

This essay traces the algorithm through the IVM-for-cyclic-joins
lens and asks one structural question: where, exactly, does the
sub-$m^{2/3}$ improvement live? Our answer points at a single
pressure point. The restricted variant (Section~\ref{sec:warmup})
reduces every per-update query to a constant number of look-ups
against stored matrix products, only one of which --- the
dense-block product $A^{L*}\!\cdot\!B_{i,DD}$ of
Section~\ref{sec:fmm} --- is a nontrivial use of FMM, and only that
one has a cost governed by $\omega$. The phase mechanism of the
full algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:phases}) promotes the restricted
variant to a worst-case bound, but the per-update budget remains
pinned to the same FMM step. Our two contributions probe this
pressure point from complementary directions.
\emph{Theoretically}, we trace the parameter trade-off into a
five-inequality constraint system and give a closed-form
certificate that the system is feasible at the conjectural optimum
$\omega = 2$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:feasibility}); the certificate exposes
\emph{which} constraint binds tight --- the rectangular FMM
constraint, the only one of the five whose left-hand side depends
on $\omega$ --- and so isolates FMM as the load-bearing ingredient.
\emph{Empirically}, we benchmark a faithful-in-shape implementation
of the restricted variant against a layer-aware wedge baseline
(Section~\ref{sec:experiments}); the comparison locates the input
regime --- bilateral hubs on both sides of the cyclic ordering ---
in which the warm-up's class-routing actually fires, and confirms
that on simpler inputs the layered baseline already captures most
of the benefit. The two angles converge: the algebraic improvement
of~\cite{AssadiShah25} lives at the rectangular FMM step, and the
surrounding machinery exists to feed that step well-conditioned
inputs.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

Dynamic subgraph counting builds on three lines of
work: static FMM-based subgraph algorithms, dynamic
wedge-maintenance for triangles, and the $4$-vertex result of
Hanauer, Henzinger, and Hua. The following sections present each
of these in turn. We then discuss why fast matrix multiplication
was long thought to be incompatible with incremental view
maintenance, and close with the positioning of the Assadi-Shah
result.

\subsection{Static algorithms for $4$-cycles}
\label{sec:related:static}
The trivial static algorithm enumerates all \emph{wedges} (length-2
paths $u$-$x$-$v$, i.e., pairs of edges sharing a common midpoint
$x$) and, for each ordered pair $(u, v)$, counts the wedges between
them; this gives $O(m^{3/2})$ time on graphs with $m$ edges and is
tight for combinatorial algorithms. Alon, Yuster, and Zwick~\cite{AYZ97} gave
the first improvement to use fast matrix multiplication: their
algorithm runs in time $O(m^{2\omega/(\omega+1)})$, which evaluates
to roughly $O(m^{1.41})$ at the current $\omega$ and to
$O(m^{4/3})$ at the conjectural optimum $\omega = 2$. The
algorithm computes $A^2$ for the adjacency matrix $A$ on the
high-degree subgraph and combines it with a list-and-count step on
the low-degree side; it was the first piece of evidence that FMM
yields nontrivial speedups for $4$-cycle counting in any setting.
Crucially, this evidence was static: until 2025, no FMM-based
speedup over the combinatorial bound was known in the dynamic
regime.

\subsection{Dynamic triangle counting and the OMv barrier}
\label{sec:related:triangles}
The triangle case is the template for this line of work. Kara,
Ngo, Nikolic, Olteanu, and Zhang~\cite{KNN20} maintain
the triangle count in worst-case update time $O(\sqrt{m})$ by
storing, for each ordered pair of vertices $(u, v)$, the number of
common neighbours $W[u, v] = |N(u) \cap N(v)|$. The number of
triangles created by inserting an edge $(u, v)$ is exactly
$W[u, v]$; updating $W$ after the insertion costs $O(\deg(u))$ for
the row indexed by $u$ and $O(\deg(v))$ for the row indexed by $v$,
and a high/low-degree partition (with threshold $\sqrt{m}$) caps
the relevant degrees and gives the $\sqrt{m}$ bound. The
OMv conjecture of Henzinger, Krinninger, Nanongkai, and
Saranurak~\cite{HKNS15} implies that $\Omega(m^{1/2-\gamma})$ is the
right answer; this lower bound holds even against algorithms that
invoke fast matrix multiplication, because the OMv reduction is
combinatorially robust. The same lower bound is inherited by
$4$-cycle counting via a straightforward reduction;
\cite{HLS22} additionally show that the bound $\Omega(m^{1/2-\gamma})$
already holds for $4$-cycle counting on random graphs, ruling out
average-case improvements through that channel. Earlier work
of~\cite{ES09,EGST12} maintained $3$- and $4$-vertex subgraph
counts in $O(h)$ amortised update time, where $h$ is the $h$-index
of the graph; this is incomparable with bounds expressed purely in
terms of $m$ and is not a direct predecessor of the worst-case
$O(\sqrt{m})$ / $O(m^{2/3})$ line of work, but it is the closest
relative on the dynamic side.

\subsection{The $O(m^{2/3})$ algorithm of Hanauer, Henzinger, and Hua}
\label{sec:related:hhh}
Hanauer, Henzinger, and Hua~\cite{HHH22} extended the
high/low-degree wedge-maintenance idea from triangles to all
$4$-vertex patterns. For $4$-cycles, they partition the vertex set
$V$ into a \emph{low-degree} class
$V_L = \{v : \deg(v) \le m^{1/3}\}$ and a \emph{high-degree}
class $V_H = V \setminus V_L$. The handshaking bound
$\sum_v \deg(v) = 2m$ caps $|V_H|$ at $\frac{2m}{m^{1/3}} = O(m^{2/3})$,
which is the load-bearing inequality of the analysis.

The algorithm maintains two indexed counts:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $W_{\mathrm{lo}}[u, v]$, the number of wedges $u$-$x$-$v$
        with low-degree midpoint $x \in V_L$, stored for every pair
        of endpoints $(u, v)$;
  \item $P_{\mathrm{lolo}}[u, v]$, the number of $3$-paths
        $u$-$x$-$y$-$v$ with two low-degree midpoints
        $x, y \in V_L$, stored for every pair of endpoints.
\end{itemize}
On an update incident to $u$, the affected entries of $W_{\mathrm{lo}}$
are those of the form $W_{\mathrm{lo}}[w, v]$ for a low-degree
$w \in N(u) \cap V_L$ and a neighbour $v$ of $w$; iterating the
inner pair costs at most
$|N(u)| \cdot \max_{w \in V_L} |N(w)| \le n \cdot m^{1/3}$
in the trivial reading, but only the low-degree neighbours of $w$
contribute new wedges, and there are at most $m^{1/3}$ of them, so
the work reduces to
$|N(u)| \cdot m^{1/3} \le m^{1/3} \cdot m^{1/3} = m^{2/3}$ when
$u \in V_L$ and to $|N(u)| \cdot m^{1/3}$ in general. A symmetric
analysis on $P_{\mathrm{lolo}}$ keeps it within the same
$O(m^{2/3})$ budget. Wedges through high-degree midpoints are
maintained only between pairs of high-degree endpoints; there are
$|V_H|^2 = O(m^{4/3})$ such pairs, but the per-update changes are
confined to the at most $|V_H| = O(m^{2/3})$ pairs that share an
endpoint with the updated edge.

Queries case-split on whether each midpoint of the counted
$3$-path is low or high. The bottleneck case, with two high-degree
midpoints and at least one low-degree endpoint $u$, requires
iterating over $N(u)$, which has size at most $m^{1/3}$, and
combining stored counts; the inner step iterates over high-degree
vertices, of which there are $O(m^{2/3})$. Either iteration alone
gives a query cost of $O(m^{2/3})$, matching the maintenance
budget.

This decomposition suggests that $O(m^{2/3})$ should be tight:
$4$-cycles would sit exactly at the geometric mean of triangles
and $4$-cliques, and any further improvement would require a
fundamentally different technique. Assadi-Shah~\cite{AssadiShah25}
show that this reading is mistaken: algebraic FMM techniques can
push the upper bound below $m^{2/3}$.

\subsection{Why FMM was thought not to help in IVM}
\label{sec:related:fmm-belief}
Static FMM-based subgraph counting~\cite{AYZ97} requires both factors
of the product, typically two slices of the adjacency matrix, to
be assembled before the multiplication is invoked. In the fully
dynamic regime the matrices are themselves the moving objects: each
edge update changes a single entry, and waiting until enough updates
accumulate to make a batched FMM call worthwhile only gives an
\emph{amortised} bound, not a worst-case one. The
prevailing intuition was therefore that any FMM-based speedup must
be amortised, and could not deliver a worst-case improvement of the
kind we discuss~\cite[\S1]{AssadiShah25}.

Assadi and Shah~\cite{AssadiShah25} circumvent this intuition by
introducing \emph{phases}: contiguous blocks of $m^{1-\delta}$
consecutive updates, for a constant $\delta > 0$. During a phase,
queries are answered by a slower routine that uses precomputed
products from the \emph{previous} phase together with on-the-fly
computation over the in-progress phase; in parallel, the FMM
products needed for the \emph{current} phase are computed
incrementally, with the per-update share of the precomputation
budgeted at $O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon})$ work. By the end of the phase,
the precomputed products are ready, and the next phase reuses them
as its previous-phase data. Because both the per-update share of
precomputation and the slower routine respect an
$O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon})$ ceiling on every individual update, not
merely on average, the bound is genuinely worst-case
(Section~\ref{sec:prelim:dynamic}) rather than amortised.

\subsection{Overview of the Assadi-Shah algorithm}
\label{sec:related:positioning}
The full Assadi-Shah algorithm runs on a $4$-layered graph, groups
the vertices in each layer into many degree classes (high, medium,
low in the outer layers; dense, sparse in the inner layers), and
precomputes one rectangular FMM product per pair of compatible
classes. A query then composes a constant number of stored entries
plus a small-degree iteration. The $\varepsilon$ in the exponent is
the maximum value for which the system of constraints (one per
class pair, expressing that both setup cost and per-query cost stay
within the budget) is feasible at the prevailing rectangular
FMM exponents. The argument also requires two simplifying
assumptions which the paper later discharges: that every layer
contains exactly $m^{2/3}$ vertices (no \emph{tiny} vertices), and
that vertices do not migrate between degree classes during a
phase.

We cover the conceptual core: the reduction from general graphs to
$4$-layered graphs (Section~\ref{sec:reduction}), the simple $O(n)$
baseline (Section~\ref{sec:simple}), which also serves as the
algorithm we benchmark, and the restricted variant with two frozen
relations (Sections~\ref{sec:warmup}--\ref{sec:phases}) whose
rectangular FMM step we develop in full and whose constraint
system we verify in closed form. The de-assumption machinery (tiny
vertices and vertex-class transitions) is left out of scope.

\subsection{Graphs and subgraph notation}
\label{sec:prelim:notation}
%% SECTIONS.md §2.1. Paper §2.1 p.6.
%% Define G=(V,E), n, m, deg(v), N(v), k-path, k-cycle, wedge, 4-layered graph,
%% layered 4-cycle.
Let $G = (V, E)$ be a simple, undirected, unweighted graph with no
self-loops or parallel edges. We write $n = |V|$ and $m = |E|$.
For a vertex $v \in V$, $\deg(v)$ denotes its degree and
$N(v) = \{u : \{u,v\} \in E\}$ its neighborhood.

A \emph{$k$-path} is a sequence of $k+1$ distinct vertices
$(v_0, v_1, \dots, v_k)$ with $\{v_i, v_{i+1}\} \in E$ for all
$0 \le i < k$. A \emph{$k$-cycle} is a sequence
$(v_0, \dots, v_{k-1})$ of $k$ distinct vertices with
$\{v_i, v_{i+1\bmod k}\} \in E$ for all $0 \le i < k$.
A \emph{wedge} is a $2$-path $(u, x, v)$, equivalently a pair of
edges $\{u, x\}, \{x, v\} \in E$ sharing the common midpoint $x$
with distinct endpoints $u \neq v$. We write $W[u, v]$ for the
number of wedges between $u$ and $v$.

A \emph{$4$-layered graph} is a graph whose vertex set partitions as
$V = L_1 \cup L_2 \cup L_3 \cup L_4$, with each $L_i$ an independent set
and edges only between consecutive layers $L_i, L_{i+1}$ (and between
$L_4$ and $L_1$, closing the cycle). The four edge sets are encoded by
biadjacency matrices $A \subseteq L_1 \times L_2$, $B \subseteq L_2 \times L_3$,
$C \subseteq L_3 \times L_4$, and $D \subseteq L_4 \times L_1$.
A \emph{layered $k$-path} (resp.\ \emph{layered $k$-cycle}) is a $k$-path
(resp.\ $k$-cycle) with one vertex in each consecutive layer. We index
layers modulo~$4$ throughout.

\subsection{Dynamic graph model}
\label{sec:prelim:dynamic}
%% SECTIONS.md §2.2. Paper §1 p.1 "In the dynamic model..."; §1 p.3 worst-case note.
The graph evolves under a sequence of updates $u_1, u_2, \dots$, where
each $u_i$ is either $\textsc{insert}(e)$ or $\textsc{delete}(e)$ for an
edge $e$ on a fixed vertex set. Let $G_i$ be the graph after the $i$-th
update, with $G_0$ the empty graph. The model is \emph{fully dynamic}
because both insertions and deletions are permitted. Immediately after
each update, the algorithm must output the exact number of $4$-cycles
in $G_i$.

The complexity measure is the \emph{worst-case update time}: the maximum
time spent processing any single update, taken over every update index
and every legal sequence. This is strictly stronger than \emph{amortized}
time, which bounds only the average over a sequence; the headline bound
of~\cite{AssadiShah25} is worst-case.

\subsection{Fast matrix multiplication}
\label{sec:prelim:fmm}
%% SECTIONS.md §2.3. Paper §2.1 p.6 "Fast Matrix Multiplication" subsection.
%% Define omega and omega(a,b,c). Cite [ADW+25] for omega = 2.371339.
%% Note "combinatorial" excludes Strassen-style.
Let $\omega$ denote the matrix-multiplication exponent: two $n
\times n$ matrices can be multiplied in $O(n^\omega)$ arithmetic
operations. The current record is $\omega < 2.371339$~\cite{ADW25},
improving on Strassen's $\omega < 3$~\cite{Strassen69}; whether
$\omega = 2$ remains open. For rectangular products,
$\rfmm{a}{b}{c}$ denotes the exponent of multiplying an $n^a \times
n^b$ matrix by an $n^b \times n^c$ matrix in $n^{\rfmm{a}{b}{c}}$
time; the inner dimension $n^b$ is shared by the two factors,
while $n^a$ and $n^c$ are the outer dimensions of the left and
right factors respectively, so $\rfmm{1}{1}{1} = \omega$. Improved
bounds across the asymmetric range are also in~\cite{ADW25}. An algorithm is
\emph{combinatorial} if it does not exploit Strassen-style
identities; combinatorial algorithms therefore do not benefit from
$\omega < 3$.

The use of FMM in subgraph counting rests on the standard
identification of biadjacency-matrix products with path counts: if
$X$ is the biadjacency matrix between layers $L_i$ and $L_{i+1}$
and $Y$ is the biadjacency matrix between $L_{i+1}$ and $L_{i+2}$,
then $(XY)_{u,w}$ equals the number of $2$-paths from $u\in L_i$ to
$w\in L_{i+2}$ through $L_{i+1}$. Iterating the product, $(XYZ)_{u,t}$
counts the $3$-paths from $u$ to $t$ through three consecutive
layers, which is exactly the quantity that drives $4$-cycle queries
in the layered setting (Theorem~\ref{thm:lifting}). Restricting
$X$, $Y$, $Z$ to specific degree classes yields rectangular factors
whose products can be computed with the rectangular FMM bound
$\rfmm{a}{b}{c}$.

\subsection{The OMv conjecture}
\label{sec:prelim:omv}
The \emph{Online Matrix-Vector multiplication (OMv) conjecture}
of~\cite{HKNS15} concerns the following problem: an algorithm
preprocesses an $n \times n$ Boolean matrix $M$ in polynomial time,
then receives $n$ Boolean vectors $v_1, \dots, v_n$ one at a time
and must output $M v_i$ before seeing $v_{i+1}$. The conjecture
asserts that no algorithm solves the entire instance in total time
$O(n^{3-\gamma})$ for any constant $\gamma > 0$. The
$\Omega(m^{1/2-\gamma})$ lower bounds for dynamic triangle and
$4$-cycle counting (Section~\ref{sec:related:triangles}) are
reductions from OMv; we cite the conjecture only to motivate the
lower bound and do not invoke it in any proof.

\subsection{Joins as layered graphs}
\label{sec:reduction:joins}
The motivating object behind 4-cycle counting on a 4-layered graph
is the cyclic four-way join
\[
  J \;=\; A(L_1, L_2) \,\bowtie\, B(L_2, L_3) \,\bowtie\, C(L_3, L_4) \,\bowtie\, D(L_4, L_1),
\]
familiar in database theory as a fundamental hard
case for join evaluation~\cite{NRR14,NPRR18}.
The four binary relations $A, B, C, D$ over the attribute domains
$L_1, \dots, L_4$ are exactly the biadjacency matrices of
Section~\ref{sec:prelim:notation}: a tuple in the relation between
$L_i$ and $L_{i+1}$ corresponds to an edge between the two
consecutive layers in $G' = (L_1 \cup L_2 \cup L_3 \cup L_4, E')$.
A satisfying assignment $(a, b, c, d) \in L_1 \times L_2 \times L_3 \times L_4$
of $J$ is precisely a closed sequence $a \to b \to c \to d \to a$
that visits one vertex per layer, that is, a layered $4$-cycle in $G'$.
The size of the join therefore equals the number of layered
$4$-cycles in $G'$. Figure~\ref{fig:layered} illustrates the
correspondence on a small instance. Maintaining $|J|$ under tuple
updates is then exactly the incremental view-maintenance problem for
this cyclic join, which is the database-theoretic reading of our
counting task.

\subsection{Lifting a general graph to a 4-layered graph}
\label{sec:reduction:lifting}
%% SECTIONS.md §3.2. Paper §8 p.29-30 par.1-2.
%% Construction: V x {1,2,3,4}; an edge (u,v) becomes 4 lifted edges.
%% Update mapping: insertions go D, C, B, A; deletions reverse.
From a general graph $G = (V, E)$, build a $4$-layered graph
$G' = (V', E')$ by replicating $V$ in each layer:
$V' = V \times \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$, with the $i$-th copy of $V$ playing the
role of $L_i$. Every edge $\{u, v\} \in E$ is lifted into all four
biadjacency matrices: each of $A, B, C, D$ records the pair $(u, v)$
(equivalently, each layer pair $L_i \to L_{(i \bmod 4)+1}$ gains the
two directed edges $(u_i, v_{i+1})$ and $(v_i, u_{i+1})$ that encode
the undirected edge between adjacent-layer copies). The construction
is depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:lifting}.

Crucially, a single update $(u, v)$ in $G$ becomes four updates in
$G'$, one per matrix, and the order in which these are applied is
critical for the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:lifting}. We adopt the
following \emph{update protocol}~\cite{AssadiShah25}:
\begin{description}
  \item[Insertion.] Insert $(u, v)$ first into $D$, then into $C$,
        then into $B$, and finally into $A$.
  \item[Deletion.] Delete $(u, v)$ from $A$ first, then from $B$,
        then from $C$, and last from $D$.
  \item[Query.] Read off the change in the layered $4$-cycle count
        immediately after the $D$-update, between the $D$ step and
        the next of $A$, $B$, $C$.
\end{description}
In both cases, the protocol guarantees that the lifted edge $(u, v)$
is \emph{absent} from $A$, $B$, and $C$ at the moment of query: on an
insertion the edge has not yet propagated past $D$, and on a deletion
it has already been removed from $A, B, C$ before $D$. This asymmetry
between $D$ and the other three matrices is exactly what forces every
length-$3$ \emph{walk} counted by the algorithm to be a length-$3$
\emph{path} in the underlying graph; without it, a walk could revisit
$u$ or $v$ via the freshly written edge and the count would
over-report.

\subsection{Equivalence theorem}
\label{sec:reduction:theorem}
%% SECTIONS.md §3.3. Paper Claim 8.1 + proof p.30. PROOF 1 of essay.

\begin{theorem}[General-to-layered equivalence]
\label{thm:lifting}
%% Statement is ours; proof follows the argument of \cite{AssadiShah25}.
%% F1: LHS must be "walks", not "paths" -- saying "paths = paths" is vacuous.
Let $\{u, v\}$ be the edge currently being updated in $G$, and let $G'$ be the lifted graph of Section~\ref{sec:reduction:lifting} immediately after the $D$-step of the protocol (and before any subsequent $A, B, C$ step). At this moment, the number of length-$3$ \emph{walks} in $G'$ from $(u, 1) \in L_1$ to $(v, 4) \in L_4$ equals the number of length-$3$ paths from $u$ to $v$ in the current $G$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
We follow~\cite{AssadiShah25}. The forward direction is by
construction: each length-$3$ path $u, x, y, v$ in $G$ lifts to the
walk $(u,1), (x,2), (y,3), (v,4)$ in $G'$, using one edge of $A$,
$B$, $C$ each. For the reverse direction, fix such a walk and write
its underlying sequence as $u, x, y, v$. Simplicity of $G$ (no
self-loops to lift) rules out $x \in \{u, y\}$ and $y = v$. The
protocol rules out $x = v$ and $y = u$: each would require the edge
$\{u, v\}$ to be present in $A$ or $C$ at query time, but the
$D$-first ordering keeps $(u, v)$ absent from $A$, $B$, $C$
throughout the query window. The four vertices are therefore
distinct, and the bijection follows.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Consequence}
\label{sec:reduction:consequence}
%% SECTIONS.md §3.4. Paper §1 p.3, §2.2 p.7.
%% Therefore the rest of the essay can assume a 4-layered input.
Theorem~\ref{thm:lifting} reduces the dynamic 4-cycle counting
problem on a general graph to dynamically counting layered
$4$-cycles on a $4$-layered graph: the number of $4$-cycles
through a new edge $(u, v)$ is exactly the layered $3$-walk count
delivered by the lifted instance under the protocol of
Section~\ref{sec:reduction:lifting}, and the lift incurs only a
constant blow-up in the number of layered updates per
general-graph update. The restricted algorithm of
Section~\ref{sec:warmup} therefore works directly on a $4$-layered
graph with biadjacency matrices $A, B, C, D$. The simple $O(n)$
baseline of Section~\ref{sec:simple} is presented in the original
general-graph setting and uses the same query-then-update timing
idea adapted to its single wedge matrix.

\section{A simple $O(n)$ algorithm}
\label{sec:simple}

\subsection{The wedge-counting idea}
\label{sec:simple:idea}
%% SECTIONS.md §4.1. Paper §1 p.3 "Our Techniques"; Appendix A p.35.
%% Key identity: 4-cycles through new edge (u,v) = 3-paths from u to v
%%             = sum over w in N(u) of W[w,v]; under the order-of-operations
%%             rule, the bare sum is correct (no correction term).
The simple algorithm of this section operates directly on a general
graph $G = (V, E)$ on $n$ vertices, without invoking the layered
reduction of Section~\ref{sec:reduction}. The starting observation is
that an edge update only changes the $4$-cycle count through cycles
\emph{containing the updated edge}: if the count before an
insertion of $(u, v)$ is $C_{\text{old}}$, the count afterwards is
$C_{\text{old}} + \Delta$, where $\Delta$ is the number of $4$-cycles
in the new graph that use the edge $(u, v)$. Each such $4$-cycle is
the closure $u \to w \to x \to v \to u$ of a length-$3$ path from
$u$ to $v$ in the graph $G \setminus \{(u, v)\}$ (with the edge
removed), so
\[
  \Delta \;=\; \#\{\,\text{length-$3$ paths from $u$ to $v$ in } G\,\}.
\]
A length-$3$ path decomposes as a first edge $u \to w$ with
$w \in N(u)$ followed by a length-$2$ path (a \emph{wedge}) from $w$
to $v$. Let $W[w, v]$ denote the number of wedges between $w$ and $v$
in $G$. Then
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:wedge-identity}
  \Delta \;=\; \sum_{w \in N(u)} W[w, v].
\end{equation}
In principle the right-hand side counts length-$3$ \emph{walks}
$u, w, x, v$ rather than paths; the order-of-operations rule of
Section~\ref{sec:simple:algorithm} guarantees that at query time
the edge $(u, v)$ is absent from $W$, which forces every counted
walk to be a path with four distinct vertices and makes
identity~\eqref{eq:wedge-identity} hold without any correction term.
The algorithm therefore reduces $4$-cycle maintenance to maintaining
the wedge-count matrix~$W$.

\subsection{The algorithm}
\label{sec:simple:algorithm}
%% SECTIONS.md §4.2. Paper Appendix A p.35 (prose pseudocode).
%% Subtlety: insertions query then update; deletions update then query.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
  \caption{Maintain 4-cycle count via wedge matrix $W$.}
  \label{alg:simple}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Procedure{Insert}{$u, v$}
      \State $\Delta \gets \sum_{w \in N(u)} W[w, v]$    \Comment{query}
      \State $C \gets C + \Delta$
      \For{$w \in N(u)$} $W[w, v]\mathrel{+}= 1$; $W[v, w]\mathrel{+}= 1$ \EndFor
      \For{$w \in N(v)$} $W[w, u]\mathrel{+}= 1$; $W[u, w]\mathrel{+}= 1$ \EndFor
      \State add $(u,v)$ to $E$ \Comment{after query, to keep $W$ free of $(u,v)$ during query}
    \EndProcedure
    \Procedure{Delete}{$u, v$}
      \State remove $(u,v)$ from $E$
      \For{$w \in N(u)$} $W[w, v]\mathrel{-}= 1$; $W[v, w]\mathrel{-}= 1$ \EndFor
      \For{$w \in N(v)$} $W[w, u]\mathrel{-}= 1$; $W[u, w]\mathrel{-}= 1$ \EndFor
      \State $\Delta \gets \sum_{w \in N(u)} W[w, v]$    \Comment{query}
      \State $C \gets C - \Delta$
    \EndProcedure
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:simple} maintains a global counter $C$ for the
current $4$-cycle count alongside the wedge matrix $W$. Each update
performs two distinct kinds of work: a \emph{query} that uses
identity~\eqref{eq:wedge-identity} to compute the cycles through the
updated edge, and a \emph{maintenance} step that adjusts $W$ to
reflect the new edge set. Both steps touch only the rows and
columns indexed by neighbours of $u$ and $v$.

The order in which these two steps are performed is critical,
and is the general-graph specialisation of the update protocol of
Section~\ref{sec:reduction:lifting}: in both settings the new edge
must be \emph{absent} from the relevant data structure at the
moment of query.
For correctness, $W$ must not contain any wedge that uses the edge
$(u, v)$ at the moment the query is evaluated; otherwise the
expression $\sum_{w \in N(u)} W[w, v]$ would count walks that revisit
$u$ or $v$, breaking
identity~\eqref{eq:wedge-identity}. On an
\textsc{Insert}, the edge $(u, v)$ is not yet in $E$ when the
procedure starts, so the query is evaluated \emph{first}, and only
afterwards is $W$ updated to record the wedges newly created through
the inserted edge. On a \textsc{Delete}, the edge $(u, v)$ is still
in $E$ at the start, so $W$ is updated \emph{first}, removing the
wedges that used $(u, v)$, and the query is evaluated only once
those wedges are gone. In both cases, $W$ at query time encodes
only wedges of the graph $G \setminus \{(u,v)\}$, which is what
identity~\eqref{eq:wedge-identity} requires.

\subsection{Correctness and complexity}
\label{sec:simple:proof}
%% SECTIONS.md §4.3. Paper Lemma A.1 + Claims A.2, A.3 p.35. PROOF 2 of essay.

\begin{lemma}[Correctness and complexity of Algorithm~\ref{alg:simple}]
\label{lem:simple}
%% Originally \cite{AssadiShah25} Lemma A.1; restated as our own with attribution
%% in the proof to avoid the paper-report framing (F4).
Algorithm~\ref{alg:simple} maintains the exact 4-cycle count of any fully dynamic graph in worst-case update time $O(n)$, where $n = |V|$ is the (fixed) vertex count.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\emph{Complexity.} Each update touches at most $|N(u)| + |N(v)| \le 2n$ entries of $W$; the query is an inner product of length $|N(u)|$. Total: $O(n)$ per update.

\emph{Correctness.} The query counts \emph{walks} $u, w, x, v$ in $W$. Simplicity of $G$ rules out the collisions $w = u$, $x = w$, $x = v$; the timing rule of Section~\ref{sec:simple:algorithm} rules out $w = v$ and $x = u$ (each would need the absent edge $(u, v)$ in $W$). So every counted walk is a length-$3$ path, and each such path is counted exactly once. Full case analysis in~\cite[App.~A]{AssadiShah25}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Limitations of the $O(n)$ bound}
\label{sec:simple:limitation}
%% SECTIONS.md §4.4. Paper §1 p.3 "We now want to get the update time...".
%% Tight for sparse graphs; loose for dense; memory n^2.
%% Motivates HHH22 and Assadi-Shah.
The $O(n)$ bound is tight in the worst case (a high-degree update
touches $\Theta(n)$ entries of $W$) and matches the natural
$m$-dependent target only when $m = \Theta(n)$; on denser graphs
the algorithm does work unrelated to the current edge count. A
second, practical, drawback is the $n^2$-entry memory footprint:
$\approx 16$\,MB at $n = 2000$ but $\approx 4$\,GB at
$n = 32{,}000$ regardless of edge density.

These two shortcomings motivate algorithms whose update time
depends only on $m$. The first such bound was the $O(m^{2/3})$
algorithm of~\cite{HHH22}, which extends the wedge-counting idea
above with a degree-based vertex partition.
Section~\ref{sec:warmup} develops the warm-up
of~\cite{AssadiShah25}, whose update time is
$O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon_1})$ under simplifying assumptions, and which
is the building block for the full $O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon})$ bound.

\section{A restricted variant: fixed $A$ and $C$}
\label{sec:warmup}

This section presents the restricted variant of the
Assadi-Shah algorithm: a stripped-down version that already
contains the central trick (precomputed FMM products combined with
class-restricted iteration) but in which two of the four
biadjacency matrices are frozen and two simplifying assumptions
are imposed. The de-assumption machinery, occupying §6 and §7
of~\cite{AssadiShah25}, is left out of scope; we sketch the move
from the restricted variant to the full algorithm in
Section~\ref{sec:phases}.

\subsection{Setup and assumptions}
\label{sec:warmup:setup}
%% SECTIONS.md §5.1. Paper §3.1 p.7-8, Assumptions 1, 2, 3 on p.8, Lemma 3.1 p.8.
In the restricted variant, edge updates affect only $B$ and $D$,
while $A$ and $C$ are fixed in advance. By symmetry it suffices to
handle the query case where the latest update is to $D$ and we
count new $4$-cycles by counting $3$-paths from $u \in L_1$ to
$v \in L_4$ through $A$, $B$, $C$.

Two simplifying assumptions support the analysis: (1) each layer
has at most $m^{2/3+2\varepsilon}$ vertices (for a small constant
$\varepsilon > 0$ fixed below), ruling out pathologically thin
edge sets; (2) no vertex changes degree class during the algorithm.
Both are discharged
in~\cite[\S\S6--7]{AssadiShah25} and are out of scope here. The
target update time is $O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon_1})$ for a constant
$\varepsilon_1 > 0$, which is also what the full algorithm needs
when this restricted variant is invoked as a subroutine.

\subsection{Vertex degree classes}
\label{sec:warmup:classes}
%% SECTIONS.md §5.2. Paper §3.1 p.8 (H/M/L for L1,L4; S/D for L2,L3 per chunk).
%% Notation A^{H*}, etc. paper p.8.
Outer-layer vertices (in $L_1$ and $L_4$) are partitioned by degree
in the fixed matrices $A$ and $C$ respectively into three classes:
\emph{High} (degree $\ge m^{2/3-\varepsilon_1}$), \emph{Low}
(degree $\le m^{1/3+\varepsilon_1}$), and \emph{Medium} in between.
A small overlap at the H/M boundary keeps the cut on a single
threshold; details in~\cite[\S3.1]{AssadiShah25}. We write
$A^{H*}, A^{M*}, A^{L*}$ for the row-restrictions of $A$ by class,
and $C^{*H}, C^{*M}, C^{*L}$ for the column-restrictions of $C$.

Inner-layer vertices (in $L_2, L_3$) are classified per chunk
(\S\ref{sec:warmup:chunks}): inside chunk $B_i$, a vertex is
\emph{Dense} if its degree within that chunk exceeds
$m^{1/3-\varepsilon_2}$, and \emph{Sparse} otherwise. The four
chunk-submatrices $B_{i,DD}, B_{i,DS}, B_{i,SD}, B_{i,SS}$ are
obtained by restricting both endpoints by class. The Medium class
--- absent from the earlier $O(m^{2/3})$ algorithm
of~\cite{HHH22} --- is what makes room for the rectangular FMM
call: it gives the dense-block product
$A^{L*}\!\cdot\!B_{i,DD}$ the asymmetric dimensions that current
rectangular bounds can exploit.

\subsection{Chunks and lazy maintenance}
\label{sec:warmup:chunks}
%% SECTIONS.md §5.3. Paper §3.1 p.8 (chunks), §3.2 p.9 (lazy), §3.3 p.11 (lazy eval),
%% §3.3 p.12 (negative-edge subtlety).
%% State Lemma 3.2 (worst-case time accounting).
$B$-updates are grouped into \emph{chunks} of size
$m^{2/3-\varepsilon_1}$; we write
$B_{<i} := \sum_{j<i} B_j$ for the aggregate of all completed
chunks. Cheap bookkeeping is maintained eagerly. The expensive
matrix products are not per-update affordable, so they are
recomputed once per chunk: the products needed for $B_{<i+1}$ are
built during the $m^{2/3-\varepsilon_1}$ updates of chunk
$B_{i+1}$ itself, with a total budget of
$O(m^{4/3-2\varepsilon_1})$ --- i.e., $O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon_1})$
per update.

While inside chunk $B_{i+1}$, queries combine the previous-chunk
products with a \emph{lazy} edge-by-edge scan of the active
chunks $B_i, B_{i+1}$, costing $O(1)$ per edge. Edges deleted in a
later chunk than they were inserted are recorded as negative
entries~\cite[\S3.3]{AssadiShah25}, so the chunk-aggregate stays
correct without retroactive edits.

\label{sec:warmup:datastructures}

\subsection{Answering queries}
\label{sec:warmup:queries}
A query $(u, v)$ with $u \in L_1$, $v \in L_4$ is answered by case
analysis on the classes of $u$ and
$v$~\cite[Lemma~3.8]{AssadiShah25}:
\begin{description}
  \item[Both High.] $O(1)$: read the precomputed triple product.
  \item[Mixed.] $O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon_1})$: iterate the lower-class
    endpoint's row (at most $2m^{2/3-\varepsilon_1}$ entries) and
    look up two-hop counts.
  \item[Both Low.] $O(m^{1/3+\varepsilon_1})$: iterate the
    low-degree neighbourhood ($2m^{1/3+\varepsilon_1}$ entries) and
    combine the four DD/SS/SD/DS contributions.
\end{description}
Plus a lazy edge-scan of the active chunks $B_i, B_{i+1}$, costing
another $O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon_1})$. Combining these bounds with
Lemma~\ref{lem:warmup-update} yields the main statement of the
warm-up: under our two assumptions, $4$-cycles in a fully dynamic
$4$-layered graph are counted in worst-case time
$O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon_1})$.

\section{The FMM step and the constraint system}
\label{sec:fmm}

This section opens up the single pressure point of the restricted
algorithm: the dense-block product $A^{L*}\!\cdot\!B_{i,DD}$,
evaluated once per chunk transition. Every other ingredient of the
warm-up respects the per-chunk budget by counting --- the eager-update
bookkeeping, the lazy in-chunk evaluation, the threshold-class
iterations --- but this product is the only nontrivial use of fast
matrix multiplication in the algorithm, and the only step whose cost
is governed by $\omega$. We bound that cost
(Section~\ref{sec:fmm:step}), set up the constraint system the cost
must satisfy together with three threshold orderings
(Section~\ref{sec:fmm:constraints}), and exhibit a closed-form
certificate of feasibility at $\omega = 2$ that pins down which
constraints bind (Section~\ref{sec:fmm:closed-form}).

\subsection{Cost of the rectangular FMM step}
\label{sec:fmm:step}

\begin{theorem}[Cost of the rectangular FMM step~{\cite[Claim~3.6]{AssadiShah25}}]
\label{thm:fmm-step}
The matrix product $A^{L*}\!\cdot\!B_{i,DD}$ can be computed in time
$m^{\rfmm{2/3+2\varepsilon}{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
%% Dimension analysis: A^{L*} is m^{2/3+2eps} x m^{1/3-eps_1+eps_2};
%% B_{i,DD} is m^{1/3-eps_1+eps_2} x m^{1/3-eps_1+eps_2}; rectangular FMM.
We bound the effective dimensions of the two factors. The matrix
$A^{L*}$ has at most $|L_1| \le n \le m^{2/3+2\varepsilon}$ rows by
Assumption~1, and we restrict its columns to indices in $L_2$ that
are dense within chunk $B_i$. By definition, a Dense vertex of
$L_2$ has degree at least $m^{1/3-\varepsilon_2}$ in $B_i$, so the
total number of dense vertices in $L_2$ is at most
$|B_i|/m^{1/3-\varepsilon_2} = m^{(2/3-\varepsilon_1)-(1/3-\varepsilon_2)} = m^{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}$.
Hence the relevant dimensions of $A^{L*}$ are
$m^{2/3+2\varepsilon} \times m^{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}$.
The factor $B_{i,DD}$ is the restriction of $B_i$ to dense rows and
dense columns, so by the same accounting it has dimensions
$m^{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}\times m^{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}$.
The product is therefore a rectangular matrix multiplication of
shape $(2/3+2\varepsilon,\,1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2,\,1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2)$,
which can be performed in time
$m^{\rfmm{2/3+2\varepsilon}{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}}$
by definition of the rectangular FMM exponent~\cite{ADW25}. \qedhere
\end{proof}

\subsection{The constraint system}
\label{sec:fmm:constraints}

Charging the cost of Theorem~\ref{thm:fmm-step} against the
per-chunk budget $m^{4/3-2\varepsilon_1}$ from
Lemma~\ref{lem:warmup-update} yields the inequality
\begin{equation}\label{eq:warmup-fmm}
\rfmm{2/3+2\varepsilon}{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}
\;\le\; 4/3 - 2\varepsilon_1,
\end{equation}
the FMM constraint for the Low-Dense block. The High-High product
gives a symmetric FMM constraint, and three further inequalities
(threshold orderings on $\varepsilon, \varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2$
--- the overlap conditions on the class boundaries) complete the
system:
\begin{align*}
&\rfmm{2/3+2\varepsilon}{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}{1/3-\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2}
   \le 4/3 - 2\varepsilon_1, \\
&\rfmm{1/3+\varepsilon_1}{2/3-\varepsilon_1}{1/3+\varepsilon_1}
   \le 4/3 - 2\varepsilon_1, \\
&3\varepsilon_1 + 2\varepsilon \le \varepsilon_2, \quad
  \varepsilon_1 \le 1/6, \quad
  \varepsilon_1 - \varepsilon_2 \le 1/3.
\end{align*}
The first two are the FMM constraints --- the only inequalities
whose left-hand side depends on $\omega$; the last three are simple
algebraic relations among the thresholds (Equations~(5), (2), (6),
(7), (8) of~\cite[\S3.4]{AssadiShah25}, in that order). With current
rectangular FMM bounds~\cite{ADW25} the system is feasible at
$\varepsilon_1 \approx 0.0420$, $\varepsilon_2 \approx 0.1457$,
$\varepsilon \approx 0.0098$~\cite[App.~B]{AssadiShah25}. The next
subsection gives a clean closed-form solution at the conjectural
optimum $\omega = 2$.

\subsection{Closed-form feasibility at \texorpdfstring{$\omega = 2$}{omega = 2}}
\label{sec:fmm:closed-form}

\begin{lemma}[Closed-form feasibility at $\omega = 2$]
\label{lem:feasibility}
Under the hypothetical $\omega = 2$, the constraint system above admits the solution
$\varepsilon_1 = 1/24$, $\varepsilon_2 = 5/24$, $\varepsilon = 1/24$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
At $\omega = 2$, square matrix multiplication runs in input-size time,
and the rectangular exponent collapses to
$\rfmm{a}{b}{c} = \max(a+b,\,b+c,\,a+c)$, the cost of merely reading
the largest pair of factors. We verify each constraint in turn.

\emph{Threshold ordering}, $\varepsilon_1 \le 1/6$ (constraint~(7)
of~\cite{AssadiShah25}): $1/24 < 4/24 = 1/6$.
\emph{Threshold ordering}, $\varepsilon_1 - \varepsilon_2 \le 1/3$
(constraint~(8) of~\cite{AssadiShah25}): $1/24 - 5/24 = -4/24 < 1/3$.
\emph{Sparse-Sparse / Sparse-Dense iteration},
$3\varepsilon_1 + 2\varepsilon \le \varepsilon_2$ (constraint~(6)
of~\cite{AssadiShah25}):
$3\cdot \tfrac{1}{24} + 2\cdot \tfrac{1}{24} = \tfrac{5}{24} = \varepsilon_2$,
so the constraint holds with equality.
\emph{Low-Dense FMM}, our Eq.~\eqref{eq:warmup-fmm} (constraint~(5)
of~\cite{AssadiShah25}):
substitute $a = 2/3 + 2\varepsilon = 2/3 + 1/12 = 3/4$ and
$b = c = 1/3 - \varepsilon_1 + \varepsilon_2 = 1/3 + 4/24 = 1/2$.
Then
\[
\rfmm{3/4}{1/2}{1/2} = \max\!\big(\tfrac{3}{4}+\tfrac{1}{2},\,
\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{1}{2},\, \tfrac{3}{4}+\tfrac{1}{2}\big)
= \tfrac{5}{4}.
\]
The right-hand side is
$4/3 - 2\varepsilon_1 = 4/3 - 1/12 = 16/12 - 1/12 = 15/12 = 5/4$,
so the constraint is satisfied with equality.
\emph{High-High product} (constraint~(2) of~\cite{AssadiShah25}),
$\rfmm{1/3+\varepsilon_1}{2/3-\varepsilon_1}{1/3+\varepsilon_1} \le 4/3-2\varepsilon_1$:
the dimensions evaluate to $a = c = 9/24 = 3/8$ and
$b = 15/24 = 5/8$. At $\omega = 2$,
\[
\rfmm{3/8}{5/8}{3/8} = \max\!\big(\tfrac{3}{8}+\tfrac{5}{8},\,
\tfrac{5}{8}+\tfrac{3}{8},\, \tfrac{3}{8}+\tfrac{3}{8}\big) = 1
\;\le\; 5/4 = 4/3 - 2\varepsilon_1,
\]
verified by the same method as our Eq.~\eqref{eq:warmup-fmm}.
All five constraints are satisfied, so the proposed assignment is
feasible.
\end{proof}

The verification highlights why FMM is essential. At $\omega = 2$
with this assignment, two constraints bind tightly: the Low-Dense
FMM constraint (our Eq.~\eqref{eq:warmup-fmm}, equivalently
constraint~(5) of~\cite{AssadiShah25}) and the SS/SD threshold
(constraint~(6)); the remaining three (the High-High FMM and
constraints~(7), (8)) have slack. Of these two tight constraints,
the Low-Dense FMM is the load-bearing one --- the only
$\omega$-dependent constraint of the system that binds, and the
constraint that would force $\varepsilon_1 = 0$ if FMM were
unavailable. The other tight constraint is a purely algebraic
threshold-overlap condition that holds independently of $\omega$.
Appendix~B of~\cite{AssadiShah25} runs the analogous verification
with the current numerical bounds of~\cite{ADW25} and confirms a
strictly positive slack.
Section~\ref{sec:experiments} revisits the same FMM step
empirically, asking when its surrounding class-routing machinery
actually fires --- the structural counterpart to the algebraic
feasibility we just verified.

\section{Phases and the full algorithm}
\label{sec:phases}
%% Replaces the old standalone Section 6 (~0.75 page). SECTIONS.md §6.1--§6.5.
%% Paper §5 p.14, §5.1 p.15, §1 p.2.
%% F13: write "omega < 2.5 - O(eps)", not just "omega < 2.5".
%% F14: be explicit -- we do NOT prove this theorem; it is the source paper's.
The restricted algorithm relies critically on the fact that $A$
and $C$ are frozen: the running aggregate
$A^{L*}\!\cdot B_{<i,DD} = A^{L*}\!\cdot B_{<i-1,DD}
+ A^{L*}\!\cdot B_{i-1,DD}$ that drives the chunk machinery only
makes sense when the left factor does not change. Once $A$ also
receives updates, this telescoping breaks down and the FMM step has
to be redesigned.

The full algorithm of~\cite{AssadiShah25} replaces chunks with
\emph{phases} of size $m^{1-\delta}$ for a constant $\delta>0$.
The mechanism splits each phase into three concurrent activities,
all sharing the per-update budget of $m^{2/3-\varepsilon}$:
\begin{description}
  \item[Slow path during the phase.] Queries are answered using the
        precomputed products of the \emph{previous} phase, combined
        with on-the-fly evaluation against the updates accumulated
        so far in the current phase. Each such query costs
        $O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon})$ in the worst case.
  \item[FMM precomputation in parallel.] During the
        $m^{1-\delta}$ updates of the current phase, the algorithm
        amortises a single multiplication of two square matrices of
        dimension $m^{2/3+2\varepsilon}$ across the phase. The
        per-update share is $(m^{2/3+2\varepsilon})^\omega /
        m^{1-\delta}$ time, which the constraint below forces to be
        $O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon})$.
  \item[Roll-forward at phase boundaries.] When the phase ends, the
        freshly computed products become the new ``previous-phase''
        data structure, and a new phase begins.
\end{description}
Because the per-update cost of every concurrent activity is
$O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon})$ on every update, not merely on
average, the resulting bound is worst-case. The cost analysis
in~\cite[\S4]{AssadiShah25} distills to the single inequality
\[
1 - \delta \;\ge\; (2\omega+1)\,\varepsilon + (\omega-1)\,\tfrac{2}{3},
\]
which has a feasible solution with $\delta>0$ if and only if
$\omega < 5/2 - O(\varepsilon)$. This is the conceptual content of
the result: the speedup vanishes long before the cubic barrier,
and Strassen's $\omega \approx 2.81$~\cite{Strassen69} is therefore
\emph{not} sufficient. Only the asymptotically aggressive bounds
of~\cite{ADW25}, which give $\omega \approx 2.371339$, push the
exponent below $5/2$ at all. At the conjectural $\omega = 2$ with
$\varepsilon = 1/24$, the inequality binds at $\delta = 1/8$
(since $1 - 1/8 = 7/8 = 5/24 + 16/24 = (2 \cdot 2 + 1)/24 + 2/3$).

\begin{theorem}[Main result of \cite{AssadiShah25}, restated]
\label{thm:main-paper}
There is an algorithm for counting 4-cycles in any fully dynamic graph in worst-case update time $O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon})$ for some constant $\varepsilon > 0$. With current $\omega = 2.371339$, $\varepsilon \approx 0.0098$; with $\omega = 2$, $\varepsilon = 1/24$.
\end{theorem}

%% F14: the phrasing below makes it explicit that this essay's formal contribution
%% is Theorems~\ref{thm:lifting} and~\ref{thm:fmm-step} and Lemma~\ref{lem:simple}
%% only -- the full assembly (paper Sections 4--7) is referenced, not reproved.
We restate Theorem~\ref{thm:main-paper} verbatim
from~\cite{AssadiShah25} and do \emph{not} reprove it. Its proof
combines the restricted algorithm of this section, the phase machinery sketched
above, the removal of Assumption~1 via tiny-vertex
handling~\cite[\S6]{AssadiShah25}, and the removal of Assumption~2
via vertex-class transitions~\cite[\S7]{AssadiShah25}, none of
which we reproduce. The formal contribution of this paper is
limited to Theorem~\ref{thm:lifting} (the general-to-layered
reduction), Lemma~\ref{lem:simple} (the $O(n)$ baseline),
Theorem~\ref{thm:fmm-step} (the rectangular FMM step), and
Lemma~\ref{lem:feasibility} (feasibility of the constraint system
at $\omega = 2$).

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%% Owner: D. ~1.5 pages. SECTIONS.md "§7". Code in ../code/.
%% No paper map -- this section is our contribution.

Section~\ref{sec:fmm} identified the rectangular FMM step as the
algebraically load-bearing component of the restricted variant; this
section is the empirical complement, asking the structural counterpart
of the same question for the class-routing machinery that feeds that
step. The warm-up of Section~\ref{sec:warmup} avoids $O(n)$-per-update
enumeration when an endpoint is high-degree by storing matrix products
restricted to high-degree submatrices and dispatching by class. We ask
empirically: when does this class-routing actually pay off in
wall-clock time? Comparing \textsc{Warmup-V3} only to
\textsc{Simple-Wedge} (which sees the union graph and pays
$O(\deg_{\text{union}}(u))$ per update) is misleading: a layer-aware
baseline already avoids much of that cost. We therefore introduce
\textsc{Layered-Simple-Wedge}, the apples-to-apples baseline.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:experiments:setup}

We compare three counters with a common insert/delete/count interface,
all written in Python~3.11 with NumPy. \textsc{Simple-Wedge} is the
algorithm of Section~\ref{sec:simple} as written: it maintains a dense
union-graph wedge matrix $W$ and pays $O(\deg(u) + \deg(v))$ per
update. \textsc{Layered-Simple-Wedge} is the same idea, made aware of
the four-layer structure: it maintains only the wedge counts
$W_{13}[i, w] = \sum_{j} A[i, j] B[j, w]$ relevant to layered
$4$-cycles and answers a $D$-edge update by
$\sum_{w \in N_C(l)} W_{13}[i, w]$. It counts exactly the same object
as the warm-up ($|J| = \operatorname{tr}(ABCD)$) and is the
apples-to-apples baseline. \textsc{Warmup-V3} is a faithful-in-shape
implementation of Section~\ref{sec:warmup} with two simplifications,
both stated in \texttt{code/README.md}: NumPy GEMM substitutes for
fast rectangular matrix multiplication, and Assumptions 1+2 are fixed
at construction so updates that would change a vertex's class raise
an error. Chunk size is fixed at one, collapsing the chunk-Dense
class to empty.

Inputs are layered graphs built by \texttt{layered\_hub}: a
\emph{planted-hub} family with $k_{\text{hubs}} = 5$ vertices in $L_1$
at degree linear in $n$, and a \emph{bilateral-hub} family with $5$
such hubs in both $L_1$ and $L_4$ --- the only setting that triggers
$HH$-class queries in \textsc{Warmup-V3}. Both run a $D$-only stream
biased toward hub vertices ($75\%$ for planted-hub, $85\%$ for
bilateral). Per-update times are medians over $200$ updates across
$3$ random seeds with IQR error bars; build costs are reported
separately, since \textsc{Warmup-V3} pays an $O(n^3)$ GEMM precompute
that should not enter a per-update headline. A correctness gate
($61$ lockstep tests, all passing) verifies that
\textsc{Warmup-V3.count()} = $\operatorname{tr}(ABCD)$ =
\textsc{Layered-Simple-Wedge.count()} after every step of random B+D
streams.

\subsection{Two regimes}
\label{sec:experiments:warmup}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig8_warmup_vs_simple.pdf}
  \caption{Planted-hub graphs ($k_{\text{hubs}} = 5$ in $L_1$ only). \textsc{Simple-Wedge} scales linearly in $n$ (slope $0.93$), the textbook $O(\deg_{\text{union}}(\text{hub}))$ worst case. The layered baseline beats \textsc{Warmup-V3} by ${\approx}\,1.6\times$: with no hubs in $L_4$, every $D$-update is $HL$ or $LL$ and the $O(1)$ \textsc{HH}-lookup never fires.}
  \label{fig:warmup-hub}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Hubs only on one side (Figure~\ref{fig:warmup-hub}).}
At $n_{\text{per-layer}} = 2000$ with hubs only in $L_1$:
\textsc{Simple-Wedge} costs ${\approx}\,420\,\mu$s/update (linear in
$n$), \textsc{Warmup-V3} ${\approx}\,4.1\,\mu$s/update,
\textsc{Layered-Simple-Wedge} ${\approx}\,2.7\,\mu$s/update --- the
layer-aware baseline beats the warm-up by ${\approx}\,1.6\times$. The
reason is structural: with hubs only in $L_1$, every $D$-update query
falls into the $HL$ or $LL$ class. The $HL$ lookup in
\textsc{Warmup-V3}'s $A^{H*} \!\cdot\! B$ table iterates $|N_C(l)|$
entries --- exactly the same row sum as the layered baseline, but with
extra class-dispatch overhead in Python. \textsc{Warmup-V3}'s
distinguishing $O(1)$ \textsc{HH}-lookup is never triggered.

  \centering

  \caption{Bilateral-hub graphs ($k_{\text{hubs}} = 5$ in \emph{both} $L_1$ and $L_4$). Most $D$-updates now hit the $HH$ cell of \textsc{Warmup-V3}'s dispatch table; the layered baseline still pays $O(\deg_C(\text{L4 hub})) \approx O(n)$. \textsc{Warmup-V3} wins, by a factor that grows with $n$.}
  \label{fig:warmup-bilateral}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Hubs on both sides (Figure~\ref{fig:warmup-bilateral}).}
Planting $5$ hubs in $L_4$ as well pushes most $D$-updates into the
$HH$ cell of \textsc{Warmup-V3}'s dispatch table, where the
precomputed $A^{H*} \!\cdot\! B \!\cdot\! C^{*H}$ matrix yields an
$O(1)$ lookup. At $n_{\text{per-layer}} = 2000$,
\textsc{Layered-Simple-Wedge} costs $34.1\,\mu$s/update while
\textsc{Warmup-V3} costs $6.5\,\mu$s/update --- a $5.2\times$ speedup,
growing with $n$. The layered baseline must iterate
$|N_C(\text{L4 hub})| = \Theta(n)$ entries; \textsc{Warmup-V3} reads
one entry of its precomputed table. This is the mechanism promised by
Section~\ref{sec:warmup}: the warm-up's win comes from precomputed
matrix products at high-degree submatrices, and only when the input
has hubs on both sides of the cyclic ordering.

\subsection{Caveat}
\label{sec:experiments:caveat}

We implemented \textsc{Warmup-V3} faithfully \emph{in shape} but with
NumPy GEMM standing in for fast rectangular FMM (no practical
implementation of the latter exists at our scale; even Strassen's
algorithm needs $n$ in the thousands to break even with
BLAS~\cite{ADW25}). The $O(n^3)$ GEMM precompute is large in absolute
terms: at $n_{\text{per-layer}} = 2000$ it takes $23$\,s, against
$0.10$\,s for \textsc{Layered-Simple-Wedge}. Whether the warm-up's
per-update $O(m^{2/3 - \varepsilon_1})$ actually beats the layered
baseline's $O(m^{1/2})$ in wall-clock, with FMM in place, is the
central practical question raised by~\cite{AssadiShah25}; our
experiments cannot answer it because we do not have rectangular FMM.
What our experiments \emph{can} establish is that the warm-up's
class-routing mechanism, in isolation from its FMM ingredient, helps
only on graphs whose hub structure makes $HH$-class queries fire
frequently --- which is precisely the structural assumption baked
into the design of Section~\ref{sec:warmup}.

\section{Conclusion and further work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented the recent algorithm of Assadi and Shah for
dynamic $4$-cycle counting through the lens of incremental view
maintenance for the cyclic four-way join, asking one guiding
question throughout: where, exactly, does the sub-$m^{2/3}$
improvement live? We surveyed the line of work culminating in
their result (Section~\ref{sec:related}), recalled the layered
reduction that lets us study the problem on a $4$-layered graph
(Section~\ref{sec:reduction}), presented a simple $O(n)$
wedge-maintenance baseline (Section~\ref{sec:simple}), and exposed
the conceptual core of the Assadi-Shah algorithm: the vertex-class
partition with its precomputed FMM products
(Section~\ref{sec:warmup}), the rectangular FMM step that drives
the parameter trade-off (Section~\ref{sec:fmm}), and the phase
mechanism that turns those precomputed products into a worst-case
guarantee (Section~\ref{sec:phases}). The trace isolates a single
pressure point: the rectangular FMM product
$A^{L*}\!\cdot\!B_{i,DD}$, the only step in the entire pipeline
whose cost is governed by $\omega$.

Our two formal contributions probe this pressure point from
complementary directions. Lemma~\ref{lem:feasibility} is a
closed-form algebraic certificate that the constraint system at
the heart of the restricted algorithm is feasible at the
conjectural optimum $\omega = 2$, with explicit rational
parameters $\varepsilon_1 = 1/24$, $\varepsilon_2 = 5/24$, and
$\varepsilon = 1/24$. Where the source paper verifies feasibility
numerically with the current rectangular FMM
bounds~(Appendix~B of~\cite{AssadiShah25}), the $\omega = 2$
closed form makes interpretable why the rectangular FMM constraint
binds tight --- the only $\omega$-dependent constraint of the five
that does --- identifying FMM as the load-bearing ingredient of the
parameter trade-off. The supporting dimension analysis
(Theorem~\ref{thm:fmm-step}) is taken from the source paper. In
Section~\ref{sec:experiments} we located, empirically, the input
regime in which the warm-up's class-routing actually pays off ---
graphs with hubs on both sides of the cyclic ordering --- by
contrasting it with a layer-aware baseline that already captures
most of the warm-up's benefit on simpler inputs. The two angles
agree: the algebraic improvement of~\cite{AssadiShah25} lives at
the rectangular FMM step, and the surrounding class-routing
machinery exists to feed that step the well-conditioned inputs
(bilateral hubs) that justify its bookkeeping cost.

We highlight three directions for further work. First, the gap between the
$O(m^{2/3-\varepsilon})$ upper bound of~\cite{AssadiShah25} and the
$\Omega(m^{1/2-\gamma})$ OMv-conditional lower bound
of~\cite{HKNS15}: where on $[1/2, 2/3)$ does the truth lie? The OMv
reduction passes through boolean matrix-vector products and so does
not rule out further FMM-based speedups, while~\cite{AssadiShah25}
explicitly notes that a combinatorial $\Omega(m^{2/3})$ lower bound is
not yet ruled out either, so the gap could close from either side.
Second, IVM-with-FMM as a research direction: this is the first time
fast matrix multiplication has been leveraged for join-size estimation
in dynamic graphs, and the authors suggest the technique could
inspire a new class of IVM algorithms~\cite{AssadiShah25}. Where
else does FMM break a ``natural'' barrier in dynamic IVM? Other
cyclic joins, longer cycles, richer pattern queries? An adjacent
direction is approximate counting under updates: \cite{Tetek22}
combines sampling with FMM for static approximate triangle
counting, and a dynamic counterpart would close a parallel gap to
the one studied here. Third, on the empirical side, a faithful
implementation of the restricted algorithm with a competitive
rectangular FMM routine would test whether the constants
of~\cite{ADW25} let the asymptotic improvement surface at realistic
graph sizes; our benchmarks of the simple algorithm cannot speak to
this, and we view it as the natural next step.
